Timeline

3,000 BCE: The Abacus

Around 3,000 BCE a device called the abacus was used to perform mathematical calculations.

205-60 BCE: Antikythera Mechanism

In 1902, a Greek archaeologist discovered the Antikythera Mechanism, which dates back to 205-60 BCE and is the world's first analogous computer. The device was used for astrology as it was used to calculate the position of the sun, moon, and planets.

1614: Napier's Bones

John Napier

In 1614, John Napier published his work on simplifying arithmetic by introducing the idea of logarithms which considerably shortened the time it took for calculations.

…there is nothing … so troublesome to mathematical practice ...than the multiplications, divisions, square and cubical extractions of great numbers, which besides the tedious expense of time are for the most part subject to many slippery errors, I began therefore to consider … by what certain and ready art I might remove those hindrances.
- John Napier

1622: Slide Rule

William Oughtred

William Oughtred was an English mathematician who invented the earliest version of the slide rule. A slide rule is a device with movable scales by which simple calculations such as multiplying, dividing, logarithms can be carried out. William's version of the tool consisted of two scales that were either linear or circular logarithmic.

1622: Calculating Clock

Wilhelm Schickard

Schickhard combined Napier's Bones, for multiplication and division, with a toothed wheel system to add and subtract. It is the earliest known mechanical four function calculator.

1642: Pascal's Calculator

Balise Pascal

The machine was a mechanical calculator capable of addition and subtraction. Pascal invented the machine to help his father calculate taxes.

1670s: The Binary System

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

In the 17th century Gottfried Leibniz documented the binary number system in his article Explication de l'Arithmétique Binaire.

1837: The Difference Engine

Charles Babbage

Charles Babbage conceptualizes the Analytical Engine, considered the first general-purpose mechanical computer.Babbage's proposed Difference Engine was a special-purpose digital computing machine for the automatic production of mathematical tables (such as logarithm tables, tide tables, and astronomical tables). The Difference Engine consisted entirely of mechanical components — brass gear wheels, rods, ratchets, pinions, etc.

If unwarned by my example, any man shall undertake and shall succeed in really constructing an engine ... upon different principles or by simpler mechanical means, I have no fear of leaving my reputation in his charge, for he alone will be fully able to appreciate the nature of my efforts and the value of their results.
- Charles Babbage

Ada Lovelace

Ada Lovelace is considered the first computer programmer. She wrote "notes" on the Analytical engine considered to be an alogorithm to be executed by a machine.

The Analytical Engine weaves algebraic patterns just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves.
- Ada Lovelace

1843: The Printing Calculator

George Edward Scheutz

Inspired by the difference engine of Charles Babbage, he and his son, the engineer Edvard Scheutz (1821-1881), designed and constructed a machine to compute tabular differences and print the results.

1890: Tabulating Machine

Herman Hollerith

During the 1880s the engineer Herman Hollerith devised a set of machines for compiling data from the United States Census. Hollerith's tabulating system included a punch for entering data about each person onto a blank card, a tabulator for reading the cards and summing up information, and a sorting box for sorting the cards for further analysis.

1936: The Turing Machine

Alan Turing

The 1936 Turing machine is a theoretical device created by Alan Turing to understand the limits of mechanical computing and algorithms. It consists of a tape divided into cells, a head that reads and writes to the tape, and a program that directs its actions based on the tape's symbols and internal state. Although the 1936 Turing machine is a theoretical construct, it has had a profound impact on computer science and the development of modern computing.

1943: Colossus

The Colossus was a pioneering electronic computer developed in 1943 by British engineer Tommy Flowers. Its purpose was to decrypt German messages during World War II. The Colossus was the first programmable electronic digital computer and was a significant breakthrough in computing technology. It was used to break the Lorenz cipher, which was used by the German high command to send encrypted messages. The Colossus played a crucial role in the Allied victory by providing invaluable intelligence information.

1946: ENIAC

The 1946 ENIAC was the world's first general-purpose electronic computer. It was designed to help perform complex calculations for the US military during World War II. The machine was massive, taking up an entire room, and consisted of over 17,000 vacuum tubes and 7,000 diodes. It used punched cards and switches to input data and had a speed of around 5,000 additions per second. Despite its limitations, the ENIAC was revolutionary in its time and paved the way for the development of modern computers and technology.

1956: Darthmouth Conference

The Dartmouth Conference was a seminal event in the history of artificial intelligence (AI) held in 1956 at Dartmouth College in New Hampshire. The conference was a six-week workshop attended by a group of researchers who were interested in exploring the possibility of creating machines that could think and learn like humans. The term "artificial intelligence" was coined at the conference, and the attendees discussed the potential of AI in various fields, including natural language processing, robotics, and computer vision. The Dartmouth Conference is considered a landmark event in the history of AI and laid the foundation for the field as it is today.

1969: ARPANET

ARPANET was the first operational packet-switching network and the predecessor of the modern internet. It was developed by the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) of the U.S Department of Defense in 1969. The ARPANET connected four universities, including UCLA, UCSB, SRI, and the University of Utah, and allowed them to share information and resources over a decentralized network. The ARPANET used packet-switching technology to transfer data, which allowed for efficient and reliable communication between different nodes on the network.

1973: VSLI

The Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI) technology research began in 1973 and was a significant breakthrough in the field of electronic engineering. VLSI is a technique of integrating thousands of transistors onto a single chip, which significantly increased the processing power of electronic devices. The VLSI technology research led to the development of microprocessors, which are now widely used in computers, smartphones, and other electronic devices.

1983: Domain Name System (DNS)

The Domain Name System (DNS) was introduced in 1983 and is a critical component of the internet infrastructure. The DNS is responsible for translating human-readable domain names, such as www.example.com, into IP addresses that computers can understand. The DNS operates as a distributed database that stores information about domain names and their associated IP addresses.

1990 World Wide Web

The World Wide Web (WWW) was created in 1990 by Sir Tim Berners-Lee while he was working at CERN in Switzerland. The WWW is an interconnected system of hypertext documents that are accessed over the internet using a web browser. The web is based on the HTTP protocol, which allows for the transfer of data between web servers and clients. The WWW was designed to be a decentralized system that would allow people to share and access information globally.

2010: Deep Learning Resurgence

The Deep Learning Resurgence began in 2010 and was a significant breakthrough in the field of artificial intelligence (AI). Deep learning is a subfield of machine learning that uses artificial neural networks to model and solve complex problems. The resurgence was led by a group of researchers, including Geoffrey Hinton, Yann LeCun, and Yoshua Bengio, who developed new algorithms and techniques that significantly improved the performance of deep learning models. The deep learning resurgence paved the way for breakthroughs in computer vision, speech recognition, natural language processing, and other applications of AI. Deep learning has since become a dominant approach in the field of AI and has contributed to significant advancements in various fields, including healthcare, finance, and transportation.

2020: Quantum Supremacy

In 2019, Google announced that it had achieved quantum supremacy, a significant milestone in the development of quantum computing technology. Quantum supremacy is the point at which a quantum computer can perform a calculation that is beyond the capabilities of even the most powerful classical computers. Google's quantum computer, called Sycamore, was able to perform a specific calculation in just 200 seconds, which would have taken a classical computer thousands of years. The achievement of quantum supremacy has significant implications for a wide range of fields, including cryptography, chemistry, and artificial intelligence.
